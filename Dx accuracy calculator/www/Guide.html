Guide to using the diagnostic accuracy calculator

You can use the calculator to compute the usual diagnostic accuracy measures, just as many other online calculators will do.

The calculator and guide assume that the user is familiar with the concepts of true positive (TP), false positive (FP), false negative (FN), true negative (TN), sensitivity, and specificity.

The calculator is intended to make it impossible for a user to obtain positive and negative predictive values for a diagnostic test without seeing that these vary greatly with prevalence.

The dependence of predictive values on prevalence is foregrounded because it is a counter-intuitive concept for almost everyone, yet predictive values are what clinicians need when they interpret test results[1, 2].

A secondary aim is to make it impossible for a user to not be aware that the design of a diagnostic accuracy evaluation study determines whether or not it can measure prevalence and positive and negative predictive values.

What is the difference between case-control and cohort study designs?

A  case-control study tests samples from two groups of people: cases who are known to have the condition and cases who do not.
A cohort study tests samples from from one group, the cohort, who are selected to be representative of the populations in which the test will be used.

Case-control studies are cheaper and easier to run than cohort studies, because obtaining the samples for testing is quite straightforward compared to cohort studies.
Case-control studies tend to be done early on in evidence development for a new test [3].

In contrast to cohort studies, it is not possible to directly estimate prevalence or positive and negative predictive values from data provided by a case-control study.

Using the calculator for cohort studies

Using the calculator to compute diagnostic accuracy measures is straightforward when TP, FP, FN, and TN are available from a cohort study.

If the data available from a cohort study includes sensitivity and specificity, and you wish to compute TP, FP, FN, and TN (and other diagnostic accuracy measures) you will also have to enter the prevalence of the condition in the target population, and the number of samples in the study. This is a constraint of "simple algebra": TP, FP, FN, and TN are 4 independent variables, so their computation requires 4 different independent data items. With this knowledge, use of the calculator should be straightforward.

Using the calculator for case-control studies

If the data available from a case-control study includes sensitivity and specificity, the calculator can be used as described above for a cohort study, with one caveat about interpretation of the 2x2 contingency matrix: the prevalence specified for the condition will be included in the computation of TP, FP, FN, and TN.
This means that these numbers will NOT be the same as the numbers in the study which provided the sensitivity and specificity data.
In effect, the calculator reverse engineers the 2x2 table that would have been produced by the cohort study that would have found the same sensitivity and specificity. This allows it to calculate the positive and negative predictive values that patients and clinicians find easiest to understand.

If the data available from a case-control study includes TP, FP, FN, and TN, you will also need to enter the prevalence and study size to enable the calculation of predictive values. If the data were from a cohort study design, the study size and prevalence could be calculated from TP, FP, FN, and TN.

Of course, you can (mis)use the calculator by pretending that a case-control study was actually a cohort study, but then you would be given incorrect positive and negative predictive values. And, if you were to look at the calculated prevalence you would see that it is highly unlikely to reflect any potential clinical population.
